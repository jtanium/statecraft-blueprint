## Government Refactoring

The software refactoring metaphor is brilliant because it captures something essential: **we can change *how* government operates without changing *what* it's supposed to do**. The core functions (national defense, justice system, public goods provision, etc.) remain constant, but the implementation can be modernized.

Examples of government refactoring might include:
- Moving from paper-based to digital processes (same outcome, better implementation)
- Restructuring agencies to eliminate redundancy (same services, cleaner code)

## 3. Implementation Mechanism - How Recommendations Become Reality

**Option A: Congressional Approval (Soft Power Model)**
- Governance Agency publishes recommendations
- Congress votes to adopt, reject, or modify
- BUT: Requires supermajority (60-67%) to reject a recommendation
- **Logic:** Default is toward expert guidance, but Congress retains ultimate authority
- **Analogy:** Like how base closure commissions worked - all-or-nothing vote
- **Pro:** Respects Congressional sovereignty
- **Con:** Congress might just ignore them entirely

**Option B: Mandatory Consideration with Sunset (Structured Deliberation)**
- Recommendations automatically go into effect after 2 years unless Congress acts
- During those 2 years: mandatory hearings, public comment, debate
- Congress can modify or reject with simple majority, but must affirmatively act
- **Logic:** Inertia works in favor of good governance design, forces Congress to engage
- **Analogy:** How some regulatory frameworks work
- **Pro:** Overcomes gridlock, ensures recommendations get serious consideration
- **Con:** Could feel undemocratic if Congress is overruled by inaction

**Option C: Dual-Track System (Tiered Authority)**
- **Tier 1 - Procedural changes:** Agency has direct authority (Congressional rules, agency org charts, process improvements)
- **Tier 2 - Structural changes:** Requires Congressional approval (electoral systems, major institutional redesign)
- **Tier 3 - Constitutional changes:** Requires amendment process
- **Logic:** Distinguish between technical improvements vs. fundamental changes
- **Analogy:** Like administrative vs. legislative vs. constitutional law
- **Pro:** Allows quick iteration on obvious improvements while protecting against overreach
- **Con:** Boundary disputes about which tier something belongs in

My instinct is **Option C feels most workable** - it gives the agency real teeth for technical improvements while preserving democratic control over big changes. But I can see arguments for B as well.

## Tiered Governance Framework

**Tier 1: Constitutional/Core Branches (Tightly Bound)**
- Congress, Presidency, Supreme Court

- IRS, Social Security, Veterans Affairs, etc.
- More standardized operations
- Benefit from GDA optimization but need some adaptation room
- Maybe GDA recommendations are binding unless agency can justify exception
